Why Communities?

A common theme throughout today’s talks has been that working together is key to the success of Open Research endeavour and this is where the idea of communities are important.

This diagram is from an article by Brian Nosek, one of the co-founders of the Center for Open Science and represents how to bring about culture and behaviour change.

Infrastructure is at the base of the pyramid of culture and behaviour change, you need tools such as the Open Science Framework which allows researchers to register their studies, share materials, data and output at any point to accelerate communication regardless of publication status.

But for this to be effective the user experience of using needs to be low friction to encourage adoption and many popular services integrate with each other and strive to make their tools accessible.

From the top of the pyramid is policy, which derives from funders and institutions, and defines what you have to do within the system to gain recognition.

This in turn defines the incentives that reward participants for participating in the system.

Whilst individuals can always go about changing their own behaviour, this can be challenging when the culture is to maintain the status quo, not all colleagues are keen to change their working practices. But an effective way of supporting and nurturing individuals who are seeking to change is via a supportive community as seeing people undertaking new practices normalises the behavioural changes, provides support and demonstrates that open research is possible.

Thus communities normalise behaviour andserve to accelerate the adoption of change, in this case Open Research practices, and importantly can feed up the pyramid to the top to influence and shape policy as practices and guidelines develop and mature over time.

Nosek (2019) Strategy for Culture Change

What are Communities?

“a dynamic, social grouping where members align around a shared purpose (or multiple overlapping purposes) and participate in a shared culture. Active community participation frequently aligns with a sense of belonging and affiliation to the community. In a community the flow of information is multi-directional with members able to do something as a result of being connected to one another that they would not be able to do alone.”

Nurturing a community within Sheffield and as part of the wider International community…

Diffusion of Innovation

When any new innovative technology or practice comes along you will typically encounter this sort of pattern of diffusion where there are a few innovators who develop the system, slightly more early adopters will pick things up soon after, and following them there is the early majority where uptake increases dramatically. Unfortunately there is still a large proportion of people with varying resilience, the late majority, and some with higher resilience, somewhat unflatteringly termed the laggards but that’s not meant to be disparaging.

By normalising open research practices through building an active community we can accelerate and lower the bar for adopting Open Research practices and reach the early and late majority and the stragglers faster.

And I don’t think its about making massive leaps, you don’t have to instantly become an expert in typeseeting LaTeX documents under version control which are compiled into documents and websites automatically and are available in real time. We learn best when we take small steps, one at a time.

Connecting People

  • Varying career stages
  • Range of skills/knowledge/experience.
  • Support beyond training courses.
  • Sharing knowledge between researchers to learn from each other.

When one person teaches, two people learn.

The University comprises a range of researchers in terms of career stages, early, mid and advanced

Across each of these there is variation in the skills, knowledge and experience around the different areas of Open Science, some people have experience in pre-registering study protocols, others are familiar with the FAIR Principles for Data sharing and might have experience using University of Sheffields ORDA system.

Training courses are great for getting started but typically when applying what you’ve learned to your own work or situation there will be questions and problems that crop up and this is where the support of a community comes in really helpful as you have a network of people you can contact and ask for help.

Sharing this knowledge directly between researchers so they can learn from each other is a highly effective means of normalising the practices because…when one person teaches two people learn.

The Power of Peer-to-Peer

  • Less intimidating
  • Experience > Expertise
  • Practical > Theoretical
  • Specificity
  • Participation is the key

And so communities ar really about peer-to-peer interactions which are a powerful learning tool.

It can be far less intimidating than formal teaching environments as it lowers the threshold for asking for help when it is felt that the community is supportive and encouraging.

Very often experience is more important than expertise. From my own experience knowing a system really well means that you can often overlook certain aspects that you take for granted and that someone new to a system might struggle to understand or use.

In a similar vein sharing practical experience of how to use tools is often more beneficial than deep theoretical explanations. There can often be many aspects that might be covered in theoretical material that aren’t relevant to someone getting started and only come into play further down the line.

Specificity is also important, if you’ve a highly specific question being able to ask someone in your or the wider community is much more likely to result in a relevant answer than having to trawl through a bunch of search results or long detailed documentation.

But the key to a successful community really is about participation though and peer-to-peer interactions help promote this.

There are already a number of community activities in Sheffield.

The Open Research Working Group convenes regularly to discuss and progress issues surrounding open research within the University and hold regular drop-in sessions to discuss aspects of Open Research.

Jenni Adams, the library Open Research Manager who has orchestrated this event, also organises monthly Open Research Conversations which are lunchtime seminars open where invited speakers give short talks on specific topics which are followed by discussion.

In the past Sheffield had Reproducibilitea Journal Clubs organised by Zuzzana Zagrodzka and Jim Uttley which affords an opportunity to review and discuss different aspects of Open Research.

Similarly there used to be the Sheffield R User Group which met to share experiences of using the R statistical software ecosystem for undertaking reproducible research.

Both of these groups are being restarted so keep an eye out for announcements for these launching in October and November.

Most importantly though the community is shaped by you, if you’ve an interest or need to know more about a particualr subject please come and join the community. If you’ve material on Open Research practices, that you might have shared with colleagues within your team or department please consider sharing it with the wider community within the University.

Whether that is that is pre-registration, making your data Open and FAIR, reproducible writing, using the Open Science Framework or anything else related to Open Research please come and share it with others.

The more people who participate the stronger and more supportive the community will be. And remember the community is self-guiding so its activities and direction are meant to be guided by its members so please consider get involved.
